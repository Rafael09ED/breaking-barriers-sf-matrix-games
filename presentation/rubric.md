1. Valuable Contribution to AI Safety — /40
Does this address a real, identifiable gap with clear impact and a tractable theory of change? — /15
Score
What it looks like
0–3
No identifiable gap addressed. Impact is vague or assumed. No theory of change.
4–7
A gap is described but not well evidenced. Impact is asserted rather than demonstrated. Theory of change is generic ("this will raise awareness").
8–11
Real gap identified with some evidence. Impact is plausible. Theory of change is present but could be more specific about steps or stakeholders.
12–15
Specific, well-evidenced gap. Clear and compelling impact. Concrete theory of change with identifiable steps, stakeholders, and a plausible path to effect.


Does the project have a clear audience that it delivers value to? — /10
Score
What it looks like
0–2
No audience identified, or audience is impossibly broad (e.g., "everyone", "society").
3–5
Audience is named but general. Value to them is mostly assumed rather than demonstrated.
6–8
Clear, specific audience. It is evident why they would find this useful.
9-10
Audience is precisely defined. The project is clearly tailored to their context, needs, and existing knowledge.


Does it achieve depth OR breadth? — /15
Depth = substantive, specific contribution to a well-defined problem. Breadth = scalable, adaptable, usable by different audiences or workstreams.
Score
What it looks like
0–3
Neither deep nor broad. Surface-level, doesn't commit to much.
4–7
Some depth or some breadth, but not convincingly either. Depth projects feel incomplete; breadth projects feel generic.
8–11
Clear depth (meaningful contribution to a specific problem) OR clear breadth (adaptable with little modification).
12-15
Excellent to exceptional. Deep enough to stand as a real contribution to a specific area, OR broad enough to be immediately adopted across multiple contexts.


2. Practicality / Feasibility — /20
Could someone outside this team do something useful with the project output? — /20
This includes finished artifacts, structured plans, frameworks, or any output a third party could act on, with or without additional work. A well-structured execution plan is as valid as a polished document; both score on whether they are usable beyond the team.
Score
What it looks like
0–3
Nothing usable exists. Output vague or incomplete for anyone to act on. Adds more noise than value.
4–7
Something exists but is mostly meaningful to the team. A third party can extract some value with significant support from the team or significant effort.
8–12
Usable with modest effort. A structured plan, draft, or prototype that someone outside the team could pick up and build on without much additional context.
13–17
Clearly actionable. A specific person or organisation could implement or use this today with minimal additional work.
18–20
Immediately deployable. Polished, self-contained, and ready for a real audience. A third party could use, publish, or act on it without consulting the team at all.


3. Accuracy and Epistemic Quality — /15
Does the project accurately reflect the state of AI safety and its issues? — /8
Score
What it looks like
0–1
Contains significant factual errors or meaningfully misrepresents AI safety concepts, risks, or the field.
2–4
Mostly accurate but with notable gaps, oversimplifications, or outdated framing.
5–7
Accurate and well-informed. Reflects current field understanding without significant errors.
8
Exceptional accuracy. Demonstrates genuine familiarity with field nuances, including what is contested or uncertain.


Can the project's assumptions and claims be easily verified? — /7
Score
What it looks like
0–1
Claims are unsupported. Key assumptions are hidden, unstated, or untested.
2–4
Some claims are supported. Key assumptions are acknowledged but not fully examined.
5–6
Most claims are verifiable. Assumptions are clearly stated and the project is transparent about its limits.
7
All major claims are well-sourced. Assumptions are explicit, defensible, and where contested, the project acknowledges it.


4. Novelty and Creativity — /10
Does the project bring something new — through the problem it addresses, the way it approaches it, or both? — /10
Novelty can come from the problem area (underexplored, unaddressed by the field) or the approach (unconventional execution, fresh angle on a known problem) or both. A genuinely creative solution to a familiar problem scores as well as a novel problem tackled conventionally. Both together achieve the highest score.
Score
What it looks like
0–1
Neither the problem nor the approach is novel. Similar work is known. Adds little the field doesn't already have.
2–4
Some novelty in one dimension. A slightly fresh angle on a familiar problem, or a familiar approach to a somewhat underexplored area. Competent but not surprising.
5–7
Clear novelty in one dimension. Either: the problem is underexplored and the project fills a real gap; OR the approach is unconventional and reframes a known problem. The other dimension may be conventional — that is fine at this level.
8–9
Strong novelty in one dimension with some novelty in the other, OR exceptional novelty in a single dimension. The project is memorable and adds something the field would notice.
10
Novel in both dimensions. The problem is underexplored AND the approach is unconventional. The combination is surprising yet highly relevant.

5. Presentation — /10
Note: presenting is not mandatory, but highly encouraged! Presentations do not require a slideshow, and will likely last 3-5 minutes.
How clearly was the project communicated? — /5
Score
What it looks like
0–1
Hard to follow. It is unclear what the project is, who it is for, or what it does.
2–3
Mostly clear but key elements require explanation or prompting to understand.
4
Clear throughout. The audience understands the project and its value without additional context.
5
Exceptionally clear. The project communicates itself; no ambiguity about what it is or why it matters.


How engaging was the presentation? — /5
Score
What it looks like
0–1
Flat or disengaging. Difficult to maintain attention.
2–3
Competent but not memorable. Gets the point across without generating enthusiasm.
4
Engaging. Holds attention and generates genuine interest in the project.
5
Compelling. Generates enthusiasm or curiosity; the audience wants to know more or use it themselves.
